Reader Section 01

There is a strange kind of sadness in listening to a voice that doesn't know it has become a memory.

Most people believe memories live inside photographs.

I don't.

Photographs remember faces.

Voices remember people.

A photograph can tell you what someone looked like.

A voice can tell you who they were.

Perhaps that's why I never found this job difficult.

Or perhaps I only kept telling myself that because admitting the truth would have made it impossible to return every morning.

At exactly 8:47 a.m., I unlocked the steel door marked Digital Archive – Basement Level Two.

The corridor greeted me the same way it always did—with silence.

No windows.

No conversations.

Only the quiet hum of old servers that had spent years remembering things their owners had forgotten.

My name is Aarav Mehta.

I was twenty-nine years old.

And my job was to decide which voices deserved to live forever.

Most people had never heard of Echo.

Ten years earlier, it had been one of those ambitious internet ideas that promised to preserve a person's voice forever. Instead of writing a diary, people simply pressed a red button and spoke.

Some recorded birthdays.

Some confessed secrets they could never tell another soul.

Some sang lullabies for children who had not yet been born.

Some spoke only when life became too heavy to carry in silence.

The company disappeared almost as quickly as it had arrived.

People moved on.

Phones changed.

Passwords were forgotten.

Lives became busier.

Until all that remained were thousands of silent servers waiting to be switched off.

That was where I came in.

Every week, another rack arrived.

Every week, another collection of forgotten voices disappeared into history.

I told myself it was just data.

It was easier that way.

At 9:12 a.m., I reached for the next hard drive.

A white sticker, faded with age, carried only five handwritten words.

SERVER 14 – DO NOT FORMAT

No signature.

No explanation.

Just five words.

I smiled.

People wrote strange things on machines they were afraid to lose.

I plugged the drive into my workstation.

The loading wheel spun.

Then stopped.

One user account appeared.

No profile picture.

No email address.

No phone number.

Only a single name.

M. Rao

Below it...

742 Voice Entries.

I stared at the number for a long moment.

Nobody recorded seven hundred and forty-two voice journals.

Nobody.

Aarav clicked the account.

Nothing happened.

For a second, he thought the hard drive had frozen. Then the screen slowly faded into a simple white interface, untouched by modern design. It looked as though it had been built in another era of the internet—plain buttons, grey menus, and small blue hyperlinks.

At the top of the page was a sentence.

"Every voice deserves to be remembered."

Below it were the recordings.

Entry 001 12 January 2011 Duration: 01:48

Entry 002 13 January 2011 Duration: 03:11

Entry 003 14 January 2011

...

The list continued.

Hundreds of entries.

Almost every single day.

For more than two years.

Aarav leaned back in his chair.

Who records themselves every day?

He had restored recordings from soldiers before deployment, mothers fighting cancer, fathers leaving birthday messages for children they knew they would never see grow up.

But this...

This was different.

There was no emergency.

No "final message."

Just a woman who had decided that every ordinary day of her life was worth preserving.

His eyes wandered to the account details.

Name: M. Rao

Date of Birth: Hidden

Location: Hidden

Email: Removed

Status: Archived

No photograph.

No biography.

Nothing that could tell him who she was.

Only her voice.

Outside, someone knocked softly on the glass door.

"Tea?" asked Ramesh, the sixty-year-old caretaker, holding two paper cups.

Aarav nodded without looking away from the screen.

Ramesh placed the cup beside the keyboard.

"Another emotional one?" he asked.

Aarav shrugged.

"I don't know yet."

"You always say that."

Ramesh smiled before walking away.

He wasn't wrong.

Every voice sounded ordinary at first.

Until somewhere between the words...

someone's whole life appeared.

Aarav wrapped both hands around the warm paper cup.

His cursor hovered over Entry 001.

He hesitated.

There was an unwritten rule among archivists.

You restored recordings.

You catalogued them.

You never listened unless your work required it.

These weren't podcasts.

They weren't entertainment.

They were pieces of people's lives.

He knew the rule.

He had followed it for nearly six years.

Yet something about this account unsettled him.

Seven hundred and forty-two recordings.

One anonymous woman.

One forgotten server.

And a handwritten note.

DO NOT FORMAT.

His finger rested on the mouse.

For reasons he couldn't explain...

it suddenly felt as though someone had been waiting for him to press Play.

He took a slow breath.

Then clicked.

The loading icon disappeared.

A black screen appeared.

In its centre was a single sentence.

Recording 001

Recorded on: 12 January 2011

Time: 9:42 PM

Aarav adjusted his headphones.

For a second, there was nothing.

Then...

A deep breath.

Not a dramatic one.

The kind someone takes before speaking to themselves.

A soft female voice followed.

Young.

Calm.

Unhurried.

"Hi..."

She laughed quietly.

"This feels awkward."

Another small laugh.

"I don't even know why I'm doing this."

Three seconds of silence.

"If someone is actually listening to this..."

she paused,

"...then I hope life has been kinder to you than it has been to me today."

Aarav's hand stopped halfway to his tea.

The voice wasn't sad.

It wasn't crying.

It carried something much heavier.

The sound of someone trying very hard to be okay.

"My name is..."

A burst of static interrupted the sentence.

The waveform broke apart.

The software automatically attempted to repair the damaged section.

It failed.

Aarav frowned.

The name was gone.

Only silence remained where it should have been.

He moved forward.

"...and today I turned twenty-three."

Another smile escaped her voice.

"You know..."

"I've realised something."

"The world asks us to write diaries."

"But nobody ever tells us..."

"...that some feelings are too heavy for ink."

"So..."

"I'm going to leave my life here."

"My voice won't forget me."

Aarav looked at the recording length.

There were still fifty-eight seconds left.

He didn't notice that his tea had gone cold.

"I don't know who you are."

"I don't know if anyone will ever hear this."

"But if you do..."

"I hope you don't judge me by my worst days."

"Judge me by the way I kept trying."

She inhaled softly.

"Good night..."

"And..."

"I hope tomorrow is kinder."

The recording ended.

The room fell silent again.

No music.

No dramatic ending.

Just silence.

Aarav remained still.

In six years of restoring voices, he had heard thousands of recordings.

Goodbyes.

Apologies.

Last wishes.

This...

wasn't any of those.

It felt less like someone speaking to the future...

and more like someone trying not to disappear.

He glanced back at the screen.

741 recordings remaining.

Aarav removed his headphones.

Not because the recording had ended.

Because the room suddenly felt smaller.

He stared at the empty progress bar.

Playback Complete.

He had spent years listening to strangers.

He had trained himself not to feel anything.

If he carried every voice home, he would have collapsed long ago.

That was Rule One.

Never take a voice home with you.

He looked at the clock.

9:41 a.m.

His inbox contained twelve pending restoration requests.

A family waiting for a mother's lullaby.

A son hoping to hear his father's final blessing.

A wife desperate to recover the last voicemail her husband had ever recorded.

Real people.

Real deadlines.

He closed the recording.

Or at least...

he tried to.

A pop-up appeared.

Playback interrupted.

Would you like to continue from 01:48?

He frowned.

That made no sense.

The recording was only one minute and forty-eight seconds long.

There was nothing left to continue.

He clicked No.

The message disappeared.

Another appeared immediately.

Unfinished recordings detected.

Aarav sat up.

Unfinished?

He opened the account diagnostics.

Every recording displayed a green tick.

Except the first.

Instead of a checkmark...

it showed a tiny yellow triangle.

Possible data loss.

He opened the metadata.

Nothing unusual.

File size.

Creation date.

Compression history.

Then one final line.

His eyes stopped moving.

Last accessed:

18 October 2022 – 9:17 a.m.

He checked the clock again.

9:17 a.m.

That was impossible.

He had plugged the hard drive into his computer at exactly 9:12.

No one else had entered the room.

No one else had opened the file.

Yet the system insisted someone had listened to the recording...

five minutes before he had.

He refreshed the page.

The timestamp remained.

His pulse quickened.

"A software bug," he whispered.

It had to be.

Old systems behaved strangely all the time.

Corrupted databases.

Broken timestamps.

Wrong time zones.

He had seen everything.

Almost everything.

Still...

he couldn't explain why the room suddenly felt occupied.

He slowly turned around.

Rows of silent servers blinked in the darkness.

Thousands of tiny green lights.

Like distant stars.

Watching.

A notification chimed.

Not from his computer.

From the old server itself.

One line appeared on the screen.

Entry 002 is waiting.

Aarav blinked.

Waiting?

No archive software used language like that.

He checked the code.

There was no automatic prompt.

No scheduled playback.

No hidden script.

Nothing.

Yet the sentence remained.

Quietly.

Patiently.

Almost...

as if someone on the other side already knew he wasn't going to stop at one recording.

Aarav looked at the screen for several seconds before moving the mouse.

He clicked outside the notification.

Nothing.

It remained.

Entry 002 is waiting.

He opened the program log.

Every action performed by the archive software was recorded there.

09:12:14 — Server connected.

09:13:01 — User authenticated.

09:17:43 — Recording 001 accessed.

Only one access.

His access.

The strange timestamp had disappeared.

He leaned back and let out a slow breath.

"So it was a bug."

The words sounded convincing.

His heartbeat didn't.

He closed the diagnostics and opened the next recording.

Before pressing play, he noticed something he had somehow missed.

Each recording had a title.

Not numbers.

Titles.

001 — Today Felt Heavy

002 — The Boy at the Bus Stop

003 — Things I Never Tell Anyone

004 — Rain Smells Different Here

005 — I Lied Today

He smiled without realizing it.

Nobody named diary entries like that anymore.

Every title felt like the first sentence of a story.

He scrolled.

Hundreds of them.

041 — I Think I'm Falling Behind

087 — My Mother Doesn't Know

154 — Today I Almost Quit

208 — I Finally Forgave Him

319 — I Saw Snow for the First Time

441 — If You're Still Listening... Thank You

He stopped scrolling.

That title didn't belong there.

If You're Still Listening... Thank You.

Listening?

She expected someone to hear these?

No.

That couldn't be right.

Recording 001 clearly suggested she believed no one ever would.

Something had changed.

Somewhere between the first recording and the four hundred and forty-first...

she had started speaking as though someone was accompanying her.

Not talking to her.

Just...

listening.

Aarav closed his eyes for a moment.

"This isn't healthy," he muttered.

He was becoming curious.

Curiosity was dangerous in his profession.

Curiosity made archivists forget that these were real lives, not stories.

His phone vibrated.

MOTHER CALLING

He answered immediately.

"Good morning, Ma."

"Did you have breakfast?"

"No."

"I knew it."

Aarav smiled.

"You always know."

"I don't always know," she replied softly. "I just know you."

There was a brief silence.

Then she asked,

"Are you coming home this weekend?"

"I'll try."

"You said that last weekend too."

"I know."

"Your father pretends he doesn't wait, but he keeps asking when you'll come."

"I'll be there."

"You promise?"

He looked at the monitor.

At the endless list of recordings.

"I promise."

After the call ended, the room felt strangely quiet again.

He remembered something his father had once told him.

"One day, son, people won't remember what we looked like.

But they'll remember how our voice made them feel."

At the time, Aarav had laughed.

Today...

he wasn't laughing.

His cursor moved back to the screen.

Without thinking.

Without planning.

Almost by instinct.

It rested over the second recording.

The Boy at the Bus Stop.

He clicked.

The progress bar began to move.

And somewhere, twelve years earlier...

a young woman took a deep breath and began speaking again.

A soft click echoed through Aarav's headphones.

This recording began differently.

No deep breath.

No awkward laugh.

Just the distant sound of rain.

Not heavy rain.

The kind that falls quietly after sunset, tapping against windows as if it doesn't want to disturb anyone.

For nearly fifteen seconds...

she didn't speak.

Aarav checked whether the file had frozen.

Then her voice arrived.

"So..."

she whispered.

"I met someone today."

Aarav instinctively looked at the title again.

The Boy at the Bus Stop.

"I don't know his name."

"I don't know where he lives."

"I don't even know if I'll ever see him again."

A tiny laugh escaped her.

"And before you start judging me..."

"No."

"I didn't fall in love."

"He probably doesn't even remember my face."

Another pause.

"But today reminded me that the smallest moments somehow become the ones we remember forever."

The rain grew louder.

"I missed my usual bus."

"So I had to wait for the next one."

"There was this little boy..."

"...maybe seven years old."

"He was standing beside me holding the biggest school bag I've ever seen."

"I think the bag weighed more than he did."

She laughed again.

A genuine laugh this time.

"He kept staring at everyone."

"Then..."

"He looked at me."

"And asked..."

'Excuse me... are grown-ups lonely?'

Aarav's eyes lifted from the screen.

The room disappeared around him.

She continued.

"I didn't know what to say."

"So I asked him..."

'Why would you ask that?'

"He pointed at all the people waiting for the bus."

"'Nobody is talking.'"

"'Everyone looks sad.'"

"'When I grow up...'"

"'...will I become lonely too?'"

The recording fell silent for several seconds.

Long enough for Aarav to imagine the scene.

A rainy evening.

A crowded bus stop.

A child asking a question most adults spent their lives avoiding.

"I lied to him."

"I smiled."

"And I told him..."

'No.'

'Growing up doesn't make you lonely.'

'It just makes you busier.'

Another pause.

"So..."

"If you're listening to this years from now..."

"I want to confess something."

"I wasn't answering him."

"I was answering myself."

The rain slowly faded.

"I've been busy for years."

"College."

"Work."

"Family."

"Expectations."

"Plans."

"So busy..."

"...that somewhere along the way..."

"...I forgot how to make new memories."

Aarav swallowed.

There was something unsettling about the way she spoke.

Not because she sounded broken.

Because she sounded ordinary.

Like someone he could have walked past a thousand times without ever noticing.

Then came one final sentence.

"So if I ever become too busy to remember today..."

"I hope this recording reminds me..."

"...that a seven-year-old once understood adulthood better than I ever did."

A soft beep signaled the end.

The waveform disappeared.

For the first time that morning, Aarav didn't move.

He simply sat there.

Listening...

to the silence she had left behind.

The office slowly came back to life.

Printers hummed.

Telephones rang somewhere beyond the archive room.

Someone laughed in the hallway.

The ordinary world had returned.

But Aarav hadn't.

He removed his headphones and rubbed his eyes.

His monitor still displayed the timeline of the recording.

Playback Complete.

Duration:

03:11

Three minutes.

Three minutes had somehow felt longer than most conversations he had in an entire week.

A soft knock interrupted his thoughts.

"Busy?" asked Nisha.

She stood at the doorway with two files tucked under her arm and a half-finished cup of coffee in her hand.

Nisha Kapoor had joined the archive six months earlier.

She was twenty-six.

Quick-witted.

Hopelessly addicted to coffee.

And one of the few people who still smiled before nine in the morning.

"You've been staring at that screen for almost half an hour," she said.

"I've seen stranger things."

"So have I."

She stepped inside.

"What have you got?"

"Nothing."

She raised an eyebrow.

"When you say 'nothing,' it usually means something."

Aarav hesitated before turning the monitor toward her.

She scanned the page.

Seven hundred and forty-two recordings.

Her expression changed instantly.

"That's..."

"...a lot."

"I know."

"Who is she?"

"I don't know."

"No photograph?"

"No."

"No address?"

He shook his head.

"Nothing."

Nisha leaned closer.

"Maybe she was a journalist."

"Or a psychologist."

"Or someone conducting research."

"Maybe."

"But why hide her identity?"

She didn't answer.

Instead, she looked at the recording titles.

Today Felt Heavy.

The Boy at the Bus Stop.

Things I Never Tell Anyone.

A small smile appeared on her face.

"She writes titles like novels."

Aarav looked at her.

"I was thinking the same thing."

Nisha folded her arms.

"So..."

"What's so special about this account?"

"I don't know yet."

"But..."

He searched for the right words.

"It doesn't feel like she's recording events."

"It feels like she's recording herself."

Nisha remained quiet.

Then she laughed softly.

"That's the weirdest sentence I've heard all week."

"It made sense in my head."

"It actually makes sense."

She looked at the monitor once more.

"You know what scares me?"

"What?"

"If she really recorded one entry almost every day..."

"...then whoever she was..."

"...she trusted this website more than she trusted another human being."

Neither of them spoke.

Because neither of them could prove she was wrong.

Nisha checked her watch.

"I've got a meeting."

She picked up her coffee.

Just before leaving, she turned back.

"Don't get attached."

Aarav frowned.

"What?"

"You always do."

"You restore one person's memories..."

"...and suddenly you forget to live your own."

She smiled gently.

"Just don't disappear into someone else's life."

The door closed behind her.

The room became silent again.

Aarav looked back at the monitor.

His eyes drifted further down the list.

Entry 003.

Things I Never Tell Anyone

For reasons he couldn't explain...

it felt less like opening another recording...

and more like opening someone's heart.

Aarav rested his finger on the space bar.

For some reason, he had stopped clicking the mouse.

It felt too mechanical.

Too ordinary.

As if pressing Play deserved a little more respect.

The recording began instantly.

No rain.

No background noise.

Only the faint whir of a ceiling fan.

She spoke before taking a breath this time.

"I think everyone has two versions of themselves."

Aarav lowered his eyes.

"The first one is the person everyone knows."

"The second..."

"...is the one nobody ever gets to meet."

A soft metallic sound echoed in the background.

A spoon against a ceramic cup.

"I'm sitting in my room."

"My mother thinks I'm studying."

"My father thinks I'm asleep."

"My friends think I'm watching a movie."

She laughed quietly.

"And all of them are wrong."

"I'm talking to someone who doesn't exist."

Aarav instinctively glanced around the empty archive room.

Her words felt strangely personal through the headphones.

"You know what's funny?"

"I've never been good at lying."

"But every day..."

"I tell tiny lies."

"'I'm okay.'"

"'I'm not tired.'"

"'Nothing happened.'"

"'Don't worry about me.'"

"They're so small..."

"...that nobody notices."

She paused.

"But tiny lies become heavy."

"They pile up."

"One."

"After another."

"Until one day..."

"...you don't remember what the truth sounded like."

The ceiling fan hummed steadily.

A dog barked somewhere outside her window.

The sounds were ordinary.

Beautifully ordinary.

It felt less like listening to an old recording...

and more like stepping into someone's bedroom on a Tuesday night in 2011.

"My biggest fear isn't dying."

"It's disappearing while I'm still alive."

"I don't mean physically."

"I mean..."

"...becoming someone who wakes up every morning..."

"...goes to work..."

"...comes home..."

"...sleeps..."

"...and slowly forgets who she wanted to become."

Aarav didn't realize he had stopped blinking.

There was something unsettling about how effortlessly she translated thoughts into words.

Thoughts most people carried for years...

without ever saying aloud.

Then her voice became lighter.

"So here's today's truth."

She laughed at herself.

"I still buy two movie tickets in my head."

"One for me."

"And one..."

"...for the person I haven't met yet."

"I wonder where he is."

"Maybe he's eating dinner."

"Maybe he's stuck in traffic."

"Maybe he already met the love of his life."

"If that's true..."

"I hope she's kind."

Another short silence.

Then she corrected herself.

"No."

"I hope they're kind to each other."

"That's more important."

Aarav looked at the waveform.

Only a few seconds remained.

"I don't know if I'll ever meet him."

"But if I don't..."

"I hope he still has a beautiful life."

A gentle click.

The recording ended.

Aarav removed one side of the headphones.

Not because he wanted to stop listening.

Because for the first time that morning...

he felt as though he had just interrupted a conversation that had been waiting twelve years for someone to hear it.

Aarav leaned back in his chair.

The office around him had become a blur.

He looked at the list again.

742 recordings.

He had listened to three.

Only three.

Yet it already felt as though he knew this woman better than some of the people in his own life.

He hated that thought.

Because it wasn't true.

A voice wasn't a person.

It was only a moment.

And moments had a dangerous habit of pretending they were the whole story.

His computer chimed.

New Restoration Request

He clicked it absent-mindedly.

A child's voice.

Corrupted.

The request was marked High Priority.

Normally, Aarav would begin immediately.

Instead...

he minimized the window.

He stared at the archive again.

"What are you doing?" he whispered to himself.

No answer came.

Only the soft hum of the old server.

His eyes drifted lower down the list.

He hadn't noticed another column before.

Mood

He frowned.

Every recording had been tagged.

Not automatically.

Manually.

001 — Hopeful

002 — Reflective

003 — Honest

004 — Grateful

005 — Guilty

006 — Angry

Someone had categorized every emotion.

Not an employee.

The woman herself.

Aarav opened the fifth entry.

I Lied Today

Duration:

05:26

Mood:

Guilty

His finger hovered over the mouse.

He stopped.

"No."

He closed the file.

"This isn't work anymore."

He stood up.

Picked up his now-cold tea.

Walked to the window at the end of the corridor.

It overlooked the parking lot behind the archive building.

Employees arrived.

Delivery trucks came and went.

A stray dog slept beneath an old neem tree.

Life moved.

Unaffected.

Twelve years ago...

somewhere in another city...

a young woman had spent five minutes talking to a microphone.

She couldn't have imagined...

that one ordinary Tuesday...

a complete stranger would be standing by a window thinking about her.

Aarav suddenly wondered something.

Not who she was.

Not where she lived.

Something much simpler.

Was she happy?

He smiled at himself.

"What a stupid question."

Of course she wasn't always happy.

Nobody recorded seven hundred and forty-two voice journals because life was perfect.

His phone buzzed.

A message from Nisha.

Lunch? 1:00? Don't tell me you're still talking to ghosts.

He laughed quietly.

Then typed back.

They're not ghosts.

He stared at the message.

Deleted it.

Typed again.

Yes. 1:00 works.

He slipped the phone back into his pocket.

As he returned to his desk...

his eyes landed on something he hadn't noticed before.

At the very bottom of the archive page...

below Recording 742...

below the storage information...

below the account details...

there was a sentence in faded grey text.

Almost invisible.

As though it had been hidden on purpose.

He leaned closer.

It read—

"If you're still here after the first three recordings... thank you for staying."

Aarav's heartbeat slowed.

The fourth recording had never mentioned that line.

No system notification could have written it.

Someone had typed those words.

Years ago.

Long before he had ever existed in this story.

Without realizing it...

he pulled his chair closer to the screen.

For the first time since entering the archive...

he wasn't listening because it was his job.

He was listening...

because someone, somewhere in 2011...

had hoped that one day...

a stranger would stay.

Aarav read the sentence again.

"If you're still here after the first three recordings... thank you for staying."

He didn't click anything.

He didn't move.

He simply stared at those twelve words.

It wasn't the sentence that unsettled him.

It was what it implied.

She hadn't expected someone to accidentally find one recording.

She had imagined someone listening...

again.

And again.

And again.

Almost as if these recordings weren't meant to be discovered one at a time.

They were meant to be lived.

He opened the account information once more.

No updates.

No hidden notes.

No profile.

No photograph.

Only one line under the username.

Member since: 11 January 2011

She had created the account...

and begun recording the very next day.

No hesitation.

No practice entries.

Just...

"Today Felt Heavy."

Almost as though she had been waiting her whole life to speak.

Aarav looked at the office clock.

10:18 a.m.

Forty-three minutes had disappeared.

He hadn't noticed.

He stood up.

Walked toward the coffee machine.

Halfway there, he passed the Listening Hall.

The room was lined with small wooden booths.

Each one held a pair of headphones.

Inside Booth 6, an elderly woman sat perfectly still.

Her eyes were closed.

One wrinkled hand rested against the headphone.

The other held a faded photograph.

She wasn't crying.

She was smiling.

Whatever voice she was hearing...

had found her.

Aarav looked away.

He always did.

Some moments deserved privacy.

As he poured coffee into a paper cup, Ramesh appeared beside him.

"Still on that mysterious account?"

Aarav nodded.

Ramesh chuckled.

"I've worked in this building for twenty-three years."

"I've learnt one thing."

"What's that?"

"Voices are like books."

Aarav looked up.

"You don't choose the right one."

"The right one chooses you."

He laughed politely.

"I don't believe that."

"You will."

Ramesh stirred his tea.

"One day you'll hear a voice..."

"...and it'll change something inside you."

Without waiting for a reply, the old man walked away.

Aarav watched him disappear down the corridor.

Then he shook his head.

"Everyone's becoming philosophical today."

Back at his desk, he finally opened his pending restoration requests.

He finished one.

Then another.

By noon...

he had restored six recordings.

He couldn't remember a single name.

Not one.

But he remembered hers.

Every sentence.

Every pause.

Every laugh.

It irritated him.

Professionals weren't supposed to carry voices in their heads.

Yet there she was.

Like a song that refused to end.

At exactly 12:57 p.m., just before leaving for lunch, curiosity won.

He reopened the archive.

His eyes skipped Entry 004.

Then Entry 005.

Then Entry 006.

Without knowing why...

he stopped at one title.

Entry 017

The Day I Stopped Waiting

Duration:

18 minutes, 42 seconds.

He frowned.

Until now, every recording had lasted only a few minutes.

This one was different.

Much longer.

Much heavier.

Almost instinctively...

he whispered,

"What happened that day?"

The archive remained silent.

But somewhere...

twelve years earlier...

a young woman had answered that question.

She just didn't know anyone would be listening.

Lunch with Nisha lasted exactly thirty-two minutes.

Not because either of them was in a hurry.

Because Aarav wasn't really there.

"You've asked me the same question twice."

He looked up.

"What?"

"My weekend plans."

"Oh."

"You weren't listening."

"I'm sorry."

Nisha smiled.

"You've got that look."

"What look?"

15 July 2011

The rain had started just before six in the evening.

By the time Meera stepped out of the office building on Residency Road, Bengaluru, the sky had turned the colour of old charcoal.

Employees rushed past her, holding folders over their heads.

Someone cursed the weather.

Someone laughed.

Someone called home.

She stood beneath the entrance canopy for nearly a minute.

Watching.

Rain had always fascinated her.

Not because it was beautiful.

Because it made everyone honest.

The businessman who normally walked with pride now ran awkwardly to avoid getting wet.

Children forgot their parents' warnings and splashed into puddles.

Couples stopped pretending they didn't need each other.

looked even lonelier.

Meera adjusted the strap of her worn leather bag.

Inside were a diary she hadn't written in for weeks...

Meera smiled.

"No."

"You'll get sick."

Real.

"No."

Alone.

Cars honked.

Street vendors covered their carts with blue plastic sheets.

Twelve years later...
